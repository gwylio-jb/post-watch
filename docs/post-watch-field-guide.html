What this is

Post_Watch exists because a typical ISO 27001 engagement is one part knowing the standard, one part assessing against it, and three parts producing evidence that an auditor will accept — and until now that third part lived in a sprawl of spreadsheets, Word documents and good intentions.

The app collapses that sprawl into a single desktop tool. It knows the 2022 revision cold (all ten management clauses, all 93 Annex A controls, with audit questions, typical evidence and common gaps for each). It assesses — gap analyses, risk scoring, an outside-in WordPress scanner. And as of v3.0 it produces the evidence layer certification bodies actually scrutinize: a live Statement of Applicability, a CAPA register with an enforced lifecycle, internal audit programmes, management review minutes, KPI trends, and the training / incident / asset registers.

Every deliverable exports as a branded PDF or CSV. Every record can carry file evidence. And the whole thing runs from local storage on your laptop, optionally encrypted — because a compliance tool that ships client gap analyses to someone else's cloud is a contradiction you'd then have to explain in your own SoA.

Who it's for Independent consultants and small practices running ISO 27001 engagements — especially ones with WordPress-heavy client portfolios. If you've ever maintained four spreadsheets and a Word template per client, this is aimed squarely at you.

The mental model

Three ideas explain how everything in the app fits together.

1. Everything hangs off a client

A client is a billable engagement. Risks, scans, gap sessions, SoA rows, findings, audits, reviews, KPIs and register entries all carry a client id. Pick a client in any module and you're looking at one engagement's world; exports brand themselves with that client's logo and colours. (Records that predate a client, or that you never assigned, live under a built-in Unassigned client so nothing is ever orphaned.)

2. Assessment flows into evidence

The modules aren't islands — they feed each other in the same direction a real engagement moves:

gap analysis ──► SoA (pre-fill untouched rows) gap item ────► CAPA finding (one click, carries context) scan check ──► CAPA finding (severity mapped for you) audit line ──► CAPA finding (traceable to the audit) incident ────► CAPA finding (linked both ways) everything ──► management review snapshot (frozen at creation)

The point: when something is wrong, recording it where you found it is enough. It lands on the CAPA register with its context intact, and the CAPA register is what the auditor reads.

3. Local first, always

All data lives in local storage on your machine. The optional AI features run against a local Ollama model. Scans go direct from your laptop to the target site. There is no account, no sync, no telemetry — which also means backups are your job (the app makes them one click, and can nag you on a schedule).

An engagement, start to finish

Here's a 60-day pre-certification engagement for a fictional 40-person e-commerce firm, as it actually runs through the app.

Week 1 — set up and look around

Create the client in the Clients hub — name, logo (it'll appear on every PDF), brand colours if you want their palette on reports. Then run a WordPress scan against their storefront. Ten minutes in, you have a 0–100 security score, a categorised finding list, and — usually — the first three items for the risk register.

Weeks 2–3 — assess

Open a gap analysis session scoped to the full standard. Walk the clauses and controls with the client, marking each Compliant / Partially Compliant / Non-Compliant, with notes, priority and an owner. Take a snapshot when you finish — that's your baseline, and the diff against it later is your progress story. Populate the risk register as themes emerge: 5×5 likelihood × impact, CIA properties, treatment, linked controls. Set the client's risk appetite and the app pre-fills "Accept" for anything under the line.

Week 4 — the SoA

Open the SoA tab and hit Pre-fill from gap session. Every control the assessment touched gets its implementation status seeded; anything marked Not Applicable flips to excluded. Then do the part that's genuinely yours: write the justifications. The coverage meter tells you how far you've got — auditors read every one of these, so the meter is really a "how ready am I" gauge.

Weeks 5–7 — fix things, prove it

Raise findings from the non-compliant gap items and the scan failures. Each gets a corrective action with an owner and a due date; the app won't let a finding advance without one, and it won't let you mark one verified without a passed effectiveness check. Overdue actions surface in the Alert Center without you looking for them. Attach evidence — screenshots, configs, policies — straight onto the finding.

Week 8 — the management system, on the record

Plan an internal audit over the management clauses and execute its checklist. Record a management review: the app freezes that day's numbers (open findings, compliance %, WP score, SoA coverage) into the record automatically, you minute the discussion against the clause 9.3.2 agenda, and export the minutes PDF. Define two or three KPIs and backfill a couple of months so there's a trend to show. Fill the registers — import the training log from the HR system's CSV export, list the crown-jewel assets with C-I-A ratings, log the phishing email from March.

Audit day

Export the SoA PDF, the CAPA register, the latest gap analysis report and the MR minutes. When the auditor asks "show me how you know your ISMS works", open the KPI tab and turn the laptop around.

Try it without a client On a fresh install the app offers to load sample data: one complete demo engagement (Meridian Books) with a populated SoA, CAPA board mid-lifecycle, a half-executed audit, MR minutes and KPI history. Delete it whenever — it's clearly labelled.

The WordPress scanner

Fifty-seven checks, run from the outside — the same vantage point an attacker has. Most WP security plugins audit from inside the site; Post_Watch never asks for wp-admin credentials because the interesting question is what's visible without them.

Every finding carries a severity, the evidence that triggered it, and a remediation recommendation. Most also have a plain-English explainer — an attacker's-eye narrative, why it matters, what to say to the client — because you'll paste that into an email at some point, and it's better if it doesn't read like a tool wrote it.

What a finding can do

  • Explain to my client — a local AI drafts a client-facing explanation (needs Ollama; see §17).
  • Generate fix — a config-snippet remediation for fixable categories.
  • Raise finding — pushes it onto the CAPA register, severity mapped, so remediation is tracked to a verified close.
  • Attach evidence — files stick to the check id, so a re-scan that resurfaces the same issue resurfaces your evidence too.

Scans batch (queue several domains, walk away), schedule (§16), and every completed scan is preserved with a tamper-evident hash chain — each report carries a SHA-256 over its contents plus the previous report's hash, so a doctored history is detectable. Settings has the verify button.

The risk register

A proper 5×5: likelihood × impact gives a 1–25 score, bucketed into critical 20+ high 12–19 medium 6–11 low <6. Each risk records its category, CIA properties affected, treatment (Mitigate / Accept / Transfer / Avoid), a treatment plan, an owner, a due date, and the clauses or controls it maps to.

  • Risk appetite per client — set a score threshold and new risks under it default to Accept. You can always override; the app nudges, it doesn't insist.
  • The matrix view — the classic heat grid, per client, for the workshop projector.
  • Search, filters, pagination — a 300-row register stays usable. Deleting a risk offers an 8-second undo, because meetings have elbows.
  • AI treatment drafts — the local model can draft the treatment paragraph from the risk's own fields. You edit; it types.

Threat intel & alerts

Threat intel tracks vulnerability and end-of-life data for the plugins your scans discover, so "that slider plugin is abandoned" arrives before the incident does.

The Alert Center is one derived list — never a second copy of your data. It surfaces Critical/High scan findings from the latest scan per domain, TLS certificates inside 30 days of expiry, high-priority non-compliant gap items, and (v3.0) overdue corrective actions. Dismissing an alert remembers the dismissal; extending a CAPA due date re-arms its alert, so nothing snoozes forever. The sidebar badge and the list always agree because they're computed by the same function.

The 27001 reference

The standard, made navigable. Ten management clauses and 93 Annex A controls (2022 revision, four themes), each with implementation guidance, audit questions, typical evidence, common gaps and practitioner tips.

  • Cross-reference matrix — each control mapped to SOC 2, NIST CSF and Cyber Essentials, for the client who asks "but we already did SOC 2".
  • Control metadata — type, security properties, cybersecurity concepts, operational capabilities — the 2022 attribute taxonomy, filterable.
  • Audit prep cheatsheets — assemble a bespoke checklist of clauses/controls for a specific audit day and print it.
  • Global search — ⌘K reaches every clause, control, audit question and evidence item (§17 for the clever version).

Gap analysis

A session is one assessment pass — full standard, clauses only, or controls only — for one client. Each item takes a status (Compliant / Partially Compliant / Non-Compliant / Not Assessed / Not Applicable), notes, a priority and a responsible owner. The dashboard rolls it up into a compliance percentage that follows the client around the app.

  • Snapshots — freeze the session's state under a label ("pre-remediation", "post-audit") and diff any two later. The diff is the progress narrative your report writes itself from.
  • CSV import — bring an existing assessment spreadsheet in rather than re-keying it.
  • Raise finding — non- and partially-compliant items push onto the CAPA register with their clause reference and priority carried over.
  • Evidence per item — attach the policy PDF to the clause it satisfies.
  • AI narrative — the local model drafts the report narrative from your statuses and notes.

Statement of Applicability

The single most-scrutinized document in any certification audit, kept as live data instead of a Word file that was true once.

One row per Annex A control per client: applicable? · justification · implementation status (Not Started → In Progress → Implemented → Verified) · evidence attachments. Grouped by the four themes, with per-theme bulk actions and row filters — including Needs justification, which is the filter you'll live in.

  • Pre-fill from gap session — seeds implementation status from your latest assessment, and flips Not-Applicable items to excluded. It only touches rows you haven't edited; your curation is never overwritten.
  • The coverage meter — percentage of rows with a written justification. Inclusions need justifying too, not just exclusions; the meter counts both.
  • Exports — CSV for the working spreadsheet crowd, branded PDF for the audit pack: cover with summary stats, then every control by theme with applicability, status and justification.
Why SoA ≠ gap status Gap analysis measures how well a control is met at a point in time. The SoA records whether it applies at all and the standing justification. They cross-reference — but neither owns the other, which is why they're separate tabs with a one-way pre-fill.

Findings & CAPA

Clause 10.2, operationalised: you found problems, you fixed them, you proved the fix worked. The register enforces that story's ordering.

open ──► action-planned ──► implemented ──► verified ──► closed needs owner + needs a PASSED due date effectiveness check

No skipping. A finding can't be action-planned without an owner and a due date; it can't be verified until you've recorded an effectiveness check — and if the check failed, the app tells you to revise the action and re-test rather than letting you wave it through. That ordering is precisely what an auditor traces, so the tool simply refuses to let the trace be wrong.

Findings arrive from five directions — gap items, scan checks, internal audit checklist lines, incidents, or raised manually — each carrying its source context and clause/control references. Duplicate-guarding means clicking "Raise finding" twice on the same item tells you a finding already exists instead of minting a twin. The board groups by lifecycle stage, flags overdue actions in red (and in the Alert Center), filters by client, and exports as CSV or a branded PDF register.

Internal audits

Clause 9.2 asks for a programme that covers the whole ISMS over time — so the header of this tab is a rolling 12-month coverage meter: of the 103 clauses and controls in the standard, how many appeared (and were actually checked off) in audits completed in the last year.

Planning an audit picks a scope preset — full standard, management clauses 4–10, or a single Annex A theme — and generates the execution checklist. Working the checklist auto-moves the audit from planned to in-progress; each line takes a covered-tick and auditor notes, and has its own Raise finding button that files straight onto the CAPA register, tagged to the audit. Completing an audit with unchecked lines warns you they won't count toward coverage — honesty by default.

Management reviews

Auditors routinely ask for two or more years of management review minutes covering the standard's required inputs. This tab makes that record almost embarrassingly easy to produce well.

  • The frozen snapshot — creating a review captures that day's open findings, overdue actions, compliance %, latest WP score and SoA coverage into the record. Six months later, the minutes still reflect what the numbers were, not what they've become. The record stays honest without effort.
  • The fixed agenda — nine topics mirroring clause 9.3.2's required inputs (previous actions, nonconformities, monitoring results, audit results, interested-party feedback…). You minute each; the structure itself proves the review covered what it must.
  • Decisions — a simple numbered list of what was agreed, which is what people actually look up later.
  • Minutes PDF — one click, audit-pack ready.

KPIs

Clause 9.1 wants the ISMS measured, and it's the requirement most SME ISMSs fail quietly. Two kinds of metric here:

  • Live metrics — compliance %, mean WP score, open and overdue CAPA counts. Computed from data the app already holds, never stored, so they can't drift or go stale. Zero data entry.
  • Your KPIs — define a name, unit, target and cadence (monthly/quarterly), then recording a period's value is one number and one click. Each KPI gets a trend sparkline with target-aware scaling and an on/below-target pill.

The management review snapshot pulls from the same numbers, so "monitoring and measurement results" on the MR agenda has something real behind it. CSV export for the graphs-in-slides people.

The registers

The three spreadsheets every consultant kept outside the tool, now inside it — deliberately spreadsheet-plain, because these are meant to be fast.

Training log clause 7.3

Employee, topic, date, pass/fail. Add inline, or import the HR system's CSV export — the parser tolerates the usual header chaos (Employee/Name/Staff, Course/Topic, Result/Passed…) and tells you what it skipped. A pass-rate percentage sits in the header for the auditor who spot-checks.

Incident log A.5.24–27

What happened, when, how bad. Resolving an incident asks for the root cause and lessons learned — the two fields that distinguish an incident log from an incident list. One click raises a CAPA finding from the incident, linked both ways so it can't be raised twice.

Asset register clause 5 / 8

Name, type, owner, and C-I-A ratings (1–5) editable right in the table. Criticality is max(C, I, A) and the register sorts most-critical first, because "what are you protecting?" is usually the auditor's opening question. Controls can be linked per asset.

All three export to CSV. All deletes carry undo.

The evidence vault

Any gap item, scan finding, SoA row or CAPA finding can carry file attachments — policies, screenshots, configs, certificates. Files are copied into an app-managed vault directory on disk (not into the database), keyed to their owner, so a re-scan that resurfaces a finding resurfaces its evidence. Attach once, keep forever, and the audit pack stops being an email-archaeology exercise.

Privacy & encryption

The privacy story isn't a feature; it's the architecture. Client data never leaves the machine — so the remaining question is what happens if the machine leaves you.

Encryption at rest

Optional, one toggle in Settings. AES-GCM 256 via the platform's WebCrypto, with your passphrase stretched through PBKDF2-SHA256 at 250,000 iterations. Under the hood it's a proper DEK/KEK design: one data-encryption key encrypts everything, wrapped separately by your passphrase and by a recovery code — so changing your passphrase re-wraps one key rather than re-encrypting your world.

The recovery code

Enabling encryption hands you a one-time recovery code (eight groups of four hex characters). Print it, vault it. Forget your passphrase and the code gets you back in — at which point the app forces you to set a new passphrase, invalidating the forgotten one. Lose both and the honest answer is: the data is gone. That's the deal with real encryption, and the reset flow says so before it wipes.

Everything else

  • Lock the app any time; unlock prompts on launch.
  • Enabling/disabling encryption is crash-safe (two-phase writes with boot-time cleanup) — a power cut mid-migration doesn't corrupt the store.
  • The AI features run against a local Ollama model on localhost. Prompts and drafts never traverse the internet. No Ollama, no AI — the buttons simply explain what to install.
  • Scan reports chain SHA-256 hashes so history tampering is detectable (§04).

Backups & schedules

Backups

One click exports everything as a single file; import restores it (with an honest count — a partial restore reports itself as partial, not as success). Add a passphrase at export and the backup itself is AES-GCM encrypted; import detects the format and asks. Local-first means this file is your disaster recovery — treat it accordingly.

Schedules

Three kinds of recurring job, visible and editable in one place:

KindDoes

Schedules run while the app is open (it's a desktop tool, not a daemon) and catch up gracefully after sleep.

Power features

Ask the app things

⌘K opens search. Short queries do instant substring search over the standard. But type something shaped like a question — "show me high-severity risks for Acme", "reports from last month" — and an Ask Post_Watch row appears. Hit ⌘↵ and the local model translates your question into a structured query and runs it over your actual data. Wrong guesses fall back to normal search; nothing leaves the machine.
